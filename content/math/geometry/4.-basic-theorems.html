Basic Theorems

These theorems are an example of some simple deduction that can be made from axioms defined in Section 3.

Theorem 4.1

Thesis: Two non-parallels lines intersect at a single point.

Proof: They have a single point, because they are non-parallel, if they had more than one common point, by virtue of Axiom 3.1, they would be equal, so they would need to be parallel.

Quod Erat Demonstrandum.

Theorem 4.2

Thesis: If k∥lk \parallel lk∥l and m∥lm \parallel lm∥l, then k∥mk \parallel mk∥m.

Proof: Let's assume, that k∦mk \nparallel mk∦m, and that point PPP is their point of intersection. Then lines kkk and mmm are parrallel to line lll and they pass through point PPP. By virtue of Axiom 3.6 they are equal, which means they are parrallel, this contradiction proves the theorem.

Quod Erat Demonstrandum.

Theorem 4.3

Thesis: If l⊥ml \perp ml⊥m and k⊥mk \perp mk⊥m then l∥kl \parallel kl∥k.

Proof: If line lll was not parralel to line kkk, then by virtue of Theorem 4.1, they would have precisely one point of intersection. Denoting this point with PPP we can see that there exist two different lines that pass throgh PPP and are perpendicular to line mmm, which contradicts Axiom 3.7, so l∥kl \parallel kl∥k.

Quod Erat Demonstrandum.
