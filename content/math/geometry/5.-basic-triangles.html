Theorem 5.2

(ASA property of triangle congruency)

Thesis: For two triangle △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC and △A′B′C′\triangle A'B'C'△A′B′C′ the following holds:

∣∢A∣=∣∢A′∣∧∣∢B∣=∣∢B′∣∧∣∢C∣=∣∢C′∣△ABC≡△A′B′C′\begin{gather*} |\sphericalangle A| = |\sphericalangle A'| \land |\sphericalangle B| = |\sphericalangle B'| \land |\sphericalangle C| = |\sphericalangle C'| \\ \triangle ABC \equiv \triangle A'B'C' \end{gather*}∣∢A∣=∣∢A′∣∧∣∢B∣=∣∢B′∣∧∣∢C∣=∣∢C′∣△ABC≡△A′B′C′​

Proof: Let's assume that α=α′\alpha = \alpha'α=α′, β=β′\beta = \beta'β=β′ and ∣AB∣=∣A′B′∣|AB| = |A'B'|∣AB∣=∣A′B′∣. Then to prove the congruence of triangles, by virtue of Axiom 3.10, we just need to show that ∣AC∣=∣A′C′∣|AC| = |A'C'|∣AC∣=∣A′C′∣.

Let's assume that ∣AC∣<∣A′C′∣|AC| < |A'C'|∣AC∣<∣A′C′∣ and choose such a point X∈A′C′‾X \in \overline{A'C'}X∈A′C′ that ∣A′X∣=∣AC∣|A'X| = |AC|∣A′X∣=∣AC∣. Then △ABC≡SAS△A′B′X′\triangle ABC \stackrel{SAS}{\equiv} \triangle A'B'X'△ABC≡SAS△A′B′X′. From that we have:

β′=∣∢A′B′C′∣=∣∢A′B′X′∣+∣∢XB′C′∣>∣∢A′B′X∣=∣∢ABC∣=β\beta' = |\sphericalangle A'B'C'| = |\sphericalangle A'B'X'| + |\sphericalangle XB'C'| > |\sphericalangle A'B'X| = |\sphericalangle ABC| = \betaβ′=∣∢A′B′C′∣=∣∢A′B′X′∣+∣∢XB′C′∣>∣∢A′B′X∣=∣∢ABC∣=β

We have found a contradiction. The assumption that ∣AC∣>∣A′C′∣|AC| > |A'C'|∣AC∣>∣A′C′∣ also leads to contradiction. Therefore ∣AC∣=∣A′C′∣|AC| = |A'C'|∣AC∣=∣A′C′∣.

Quot Erat Demonstrandum

Isosceles Triangles

Definition 1.3 Triangle is an isosceles triangle when it has two sides of equal length, those sides are called legs and the remaining one is called base.

Theorem 5.4

Thesis: In triangle △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC in which ∣AC∣=∣BC∣|AC| = |BC|∣AC∣=∣BC∣, then the height, bisector and median drawn from vertex C overlap.

Proof: Since ∣AC∣=∣BC∣|AC| = |BC|∣AC∣=∣BC∣ means that the △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC is isosceles, as such we know that ∣∢A∣=∣∢B∣|\sphericalangle A| = |\sphericalangle B|∣∢A∣=∣∢B∣. Let's assume that the height of triangle △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC is ∣CF∣|CF|∣CF∣. Then:

∣∢AFC∣=∣∢BFC∣=π2  ⟹  △AFC∣∢ACF∣=π−π2−∣∢A∣=π−π2−∣∢B∣=∣∢BCF∣\begin{gather*} |\sphericalangle AFC| = |\sphericalangle BFC| = \frac{\pi}{2} \\ \stackrel{\triangle AFC}{\implies} |\sphericalangle ACF| = \pi - \frac{\pi}{2} - |\sphericalangle A| = \pi - \frac{\pi}{2} - |\sphericalangle B| = |\sphericalangle BCF| \end{gather*}∣∢AFC∣=∣∢BFC∣=2π​⟹△AFC​∣∢ACF∣=π−2π​−∣∢A∣=π−2π​−∣∢B∣=∣∢BCF∣​

From that we see that bisector and height overlap.

∣AC∣=∣BC∣∧∣∢ACF∣=∣∢BCF∣∧∣CF∣=∣CF∣  ⟹  △AFC≡SAS△BFC  ⟹  ∣AF∣=∣FB∣\begin{gather*} |AC| = |BC| \land |\sphericalangle ACF| = |\sphericalangle BCF| \land |CF| = |CF| \\ \implies \triangle AFC \stackrel{SAS}{\equiv} \triangle BFC \\ \implies |AF| = |FB| \end{gather*}∣AC∣=∣BC∣∧∣∢ACF∣=∣∢BCF∣∧∣CF∣=∣CF∣⟹△AFC≡SAS△BFC⟹∣AF∣=∣FB∣​

This implies that median lines up with bisector which means that all three segments overlap.

Theorem 5.5
